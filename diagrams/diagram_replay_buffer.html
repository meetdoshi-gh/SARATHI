How it works
Each entry is a single (s, a, r, s', done) tuple. On each training step, batch_size=128 tuples are sampled uniformly at random. Transitions may come from different episodes or non-contiguous timesteps.
Why this is fine for SAC
SAC's feedforward networks process each timestep independently. Temporal ordering is irrelevant — the Markov property holds at the transition level. i.i.d. sampling maximizes sample diversity.

How it works
Episodes stored as lists of transitions. Buffer tracks total transition count. Capacity exceeded → oldest episode evicted. Sampling: pick random episode, pick random start index, slice T=16 transitions.
Why temporal coherence is critical
The LSTM hidden state is initialized to zeros at the start of each sampled fragment. If the T transitions came from different episodes or non-contiguous steps, the LSTM's temporal reasoning would be based on incoherent dynamics — producing meaningless gradients.
Standard RL libraries don't support this
Stable-Baselines3, CleanRL, and similar frameworks assume flat transition buffers. The episode-based buffer and sequence-batch dataloader were designed and implemented from scratch for this project.
Capacity equivalence: SAC uses a flat buffer sized to hold 50 epochs × 50 batches × 128 transitions. RSAC's episode buffer is sized in total transitions (same ballpark) but organized by episode, with a minimum episode length gate — episodes shorter than T=16 are discarded to prevent degenerate short fragments. The deque-of-episodes structure evicts the oldest episode (not the oldest transition) when capacity is exceeded, which is a deliberate choice to preserve episode integrity during eviction.
